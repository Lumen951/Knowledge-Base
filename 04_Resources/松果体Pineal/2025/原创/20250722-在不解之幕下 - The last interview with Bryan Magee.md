本文是一篇缅怀已故哲学家布莱恩·麦基（Bryan Magee）的追思文章，原文作者回忆了与他的会面，并高度评价了麦基通过电视节目向大众普及哲学、开启一代人思想启蒙的传奇事业与深远影响。

作者致力于寻找那些人类社会网络中的关键节点，而麦基便是其中一员。他在哲学家与普罗大众之间借由媒体搭建起桥梁，为战后一代带来思想引领与哲学启发。

最早接触麦基来自他临终前所作的最后一部作品《终极问题》（Ultimate Question），也正是这部作品促使作者去查找有关麦基的更多信息，并发现这篇麦基生前最后一次深度采访。

麦基在《最终问题》人类困境一章中提到“我们真正的挑战是不得不在这个世界中生存并且死去，不能真正的理解它，但又不能对这个事实视而不见。不管你喜不喜欢，这就是我们的困境”。麦基幼时便被人类所面临的终极问题所吸引，其中他最沉迷于思考“存在”的问题，并由此走上哲学道路。现在，麦基在他的人生末年将这一问题重新抛给我们，试图启发我们去追问人之为人至关重要的问题。

我们或许会对探讨生命、死亡、自我、世界等深刻的哲学问题感到困惑甚至绝望，因为无从回答，也不可能得到真正的解答。但是我们得以宽慰自己的是，囿于光明，终归与困于黑暗之中存在天壤之别。

**上个月，一位杰出的公共知识分子与世长辞。麦基终其一生都为“存在”这一纯粹事实而深深震撼，因为关于现实本质的问题，“显然是世上最重要、最有趣的”。**

  

我见到布莱恩·麦基时，他已年近八十九，但头脑清醒得惊人，饶有兴致地听我讲述在牛津的时光，腰部以下则已瘫痪：从很多方面来说，这都是一位理想的采访对象。对于整整一代年轻观众而言，麦基那传奇般的哲学电视系列节目是引领他们进入这门学科的启蒙之洗礼，而他本人，则是一位文雅而洞悉世情的引路人，为人们开启了一个前所未有的、既有理论思辨（theoretic abstraction）又有扎实有力讨论的知识领域——这堪称在黄金时段播出的划时代时刻。

  

“要我，我可不会依赖电视来入门任何东西，”麦基对我吐露心声，从那副厚得像是为保暖装了双层玻璃的眼镜后面眨着眼，“在我看来，电视是个完全不重要的媒介。” 布莱恩！我的内心在哭泣……这感觉就像有人告诉我罗尼其实只有一个（译注：《两个罗尼》是英国家喻户晓的喜剧二人组），或者亲眼目睹大卫·爱登堡（译注：德高望重的博物学家）踢鸽子一样。但是——只要一想起电视节目通常都是些什么货色——你又很难不认同他的观点。麦基从他的座位上朝我祖父般慈祥地笑了笑，他坐在一堆伸手可及的书本和文件组成的“小堡垒”里，手里挥舞着一本砖头厚的大仲马著作；而那台固定在对面墙上的电视，我猜，纯粹是个装饰品。

上月辞世的布莱恩·麦基，其人生履历之广令人望而生畏。他于1949年进入牛津大学，那是一个特殊的战后时期，牛津在培养其标志性的“统治阶级精英”方面不成文的垄断地位似乎有些力不从心，以至于麦基发现自己必须几乎同时开启好几项事业。他作为电视新闻记者在《本周》（This Week）栏目中拥有数百万观众，同时开启了他三十本著作的写作生涯，在瓦格纳（Wagner）和叔本华（Schopenhauer）的研究领域做出了相当可观的学术贡献，此外还一直与卡尔·波普尔（Karl Popper）、伯特兰·罗素（Bertrand Russell）这样的人物保持着友谊。他的电视系列节目《思想家》（Men of Ideas）和《伟大的哲学家》（The Great Philosophers）共花了五年时间制作，而其中部分时间，还与他担任工党议员的十年任期相重合。

如果可以选，他本想当外交大臣，但在1982年，他转投社会民主党（SDP），这是一个沉重的决定，他知道此举会让他失去议席。事实上，到了那个阶段，他早已发现政治“是我自己并不真正想要的一种生活方式……我意识到——而且我认为这是一个成熟的标志——追求权力，在某种非常深刻的层面上，是个错误。”政治生涯不过是一种年少时的抱负，在实现之后反而变得索然无味；至于电视，那个，纯粹是为了钱。“从一开始，我就在寻找不必全职上班就能赚钱的方法……但这可不太好找。”麦基很快发现，他靠兼职赚的钱，能有大多数同辈人做全职工作的两倍。“这就是我对电视唯一的兴趣：在我开始写书的时候，它能帮我赚点钱。”

  

说来也奇妙，麦基一些最不朽的成就，在某种程度上是在他本人并不情愿的情况下加诸于他的。奥布里·辛格（Aubrey Singer），那位大名鼎鼎的广播公司高管，某天在浴缸里构思出了《思想家》这个节目，之后似乎追着麦基谈了好几个月。麦基一直推辞，最终才同意与诺姆·乔姆斯基（Noam Chomsky）录制一期试播集，但条件是他保留对节目播出与否的否行使权。“我特意选了乔姆斯基，”麦基坦白道，“因为我认为，要想把他的思想向没有哲学背景的观众解释得既明白又有趣，会是难度最大的。” 事实上，“我甚至还设了一个临时的障碍。我对奥布里说，‘好吧，我或许能做成一期，但我不认为我能做一整季。’ 奥布里颇为不耐烦地回答说：‘那好吧，两期！’” 结果，这两期试播集竟成功击中了那个难以企及的目标，在追求通俗易懂的同时，丝毫没有牺牲思想的严谨性（intellectual integrity）；麦基自己都惊呆了。

  

然而，或许具有更深远持久重要性的，是《二十分之一》（One in Twenty）这本书。这是麦基在《本周》栏目就同性恋议题主持了两期备受争议的节目后写就的。该书风格直白、毫不畏缩，出人意料地成了畅销书，并“为法律的修订做出了贡献”，即推动了次年（1967年）的《非罪化法案》的出台。他再次向我承认：“这又是一个例子，就像我跟你说过的哲学电视节目一样。我本不想写那本书，是别人说服我写的”（说服他的是他的出版商朋友，那位朋友向麦基吐露自己便是一位“身体力行的同性恋者”，并恳求他写这本书。）

乍听之下，麦基用一种近乎淡然的口吻谈论这些项目，着实有些奇怪，因为这些成就，放在任何其他人的人生里，都足以定义其整个职业生涯。那两个哲学系列——其中有对马尔库塞（Marcuse）、艾耶尔（Ayer）和伯纳德·威廉姆斯（Bernard Williams）等人的访谈——在我看来，构成了一份不可或缺、价值不朽的文献：它既永不过时，又带着一种几乎滑稽的年代感。每一集的开场，都是麦基和嘉宾坐在一张沙发上，置身于一个仿佛是巨大的米色子宫、或是宇宙候诊室的悬浮空间里——一种由三个机位拍摄出来的“哲学炼狱”。麦基半藏在他那标志性的“电焊护目镜”后面，然后会对着镜头发表一段严肃的开场白，用一种充满复合从句、学者风范的语调，勾勒出将要探讨的领域。

  

当然，这个节目朴素、甚至可以说毫不起眼的制作，实际上掩盖了其高超的呈现智慧。麦基是其中至关重要的元素；他似乎常常比嘉宾本人更能掌握其思想精髓，这都成了一个经久不衰的笑谈。他曾说，自己会刻意花些功夫去掩饰他在访谈进行中对讨论加以引导和澄清的娴熟程度。因此，这些节目极好地揭示了一点：哲学的本质是论辩性的（discursive character）。后来，乔姆斯基（Chomsky）和蒯因（Quine）都曾表示，他们接受的访谈是其毕生思想最直接、最概括的陈述。

  

**麦基生活在一个“无法醒来的噩梦”之中——他的应对之道，是重读哲学经典，“仿佛性命攸关”。**

  

“这些节目‘现在是做不出来了。简直无法想象’，”麦基笃定地说。“但是，人们不能说它没有满足观众的需求，因为它确实满足了。它的收视率是破纪录的。”我想，如今的教育电视已经是一个无可救药的类型，它深陷于风格和结构的矫揉造azote，被各种噱头和叙事弧线搞得面目全非，并被“委员会决策制”扼杀了创造力——这应该不算是什么有争议的观点。“造成这种变化的人是节目制作人。他们试图把一切都变成轻松的娱乐，甚至把新闻简报都变成娱乐，我认为这抽走了电视的精髓。”

  

所以，尽管麦基并非不为这些节目感到骄傲——他当然有权骄傲，而且他的确骄傲——但从他自己的视角来看，这些似乎都只是次要的：是在他那献身于哲学沉思的一生中，一些虽说有趣、却终究是边缘的消遣。这颠覆了一种对麦基可能存在的严重误解，即将他塑造成某种具有崇高公共精神、富有老派“里斯主义”风格的教育家，仿佛他会在睡梦中都念叨着BBC宪章，并为了亲手开启蒙昧大众的民智而牺牲自己的事业。麦基从来不是靠那种燃料驱动的。例如，当“人们说他们从政是因为想让世界变得更美好，这对我来说非常陌生……我之所以想从政，是因为我觉得我会喜欢身在政界。”他首要的、务实的逻辑始终是：他需要一份“职业”（occupation）来支撑他的“志业”（pre-occupation），也就是他一生都在与之搏斗的哲学问题。

  

的确，麦基反复将自己描述为“身负”哲学问题的人，就好像在说一种心理顽疾。童年时，他会孤独而执拗地沉思时间和空间的二律背反（antinomies），就像一个脱离了根基的古人，在毫无体系、无人引导的情况下苦苦思索这些难题。在现实的经纬中，麦基处处都能感知到悖论和清晰可辨的神秘。他被这些问题所“奴役”，又为“存在”这一纯粹事实而深深震撼。他在牛津大学贝利奥尔学院（Balliol College）担任导师的经历，更加强了他的信念：哲学问题是一种需要被“感受”到的东西，它要求一种精神上的感应力，类似于对伟大艺术的敏感。

  

> 译注：里斯主义（Reithianism）源自BBC首任总监约翰·里斯（John Reith）的理念，强调广播电视的核心公共服务宗旨是“教育、资讯、娱乐”（to inform, educate and entertain）。

  

那些形而上学的难题（metaphysical puzzles）日复一日地闯入他的意识，从未间断。有时，这些难题折磨着他。有一次，在教堂礼拜进行到一半时，他突然被一种念头所压垮，即他的心智与外部世界之间存在着一道无法逾越的鸿沟，他踉踉跄跄地逃离了教堂，在一众困惑不解的同辈教友面前几乎昏厥倒地，而那些人绝不会猜到，他身体不适的根源并非生理性的（systemic），而是哲学性的（philosophic）。我向他提出，这类具有顿悟（epiphanic）力量的启示性时刻，或许是麦基洞见的主要来源。“是的，”他回答，“这个观察非常敏锐。我并不认为自己从根本上说是个智识型人物（intellectual person）。”

  

关于终极实在（ultimate reality）的本质以及我们能对此认知多少的问题，在麦基看来，“显然是世上最重要、最有趣的问题”，他说道，“在我内心深处，我真的不明白为什么不是每个人都这么看。”诉诸于常识和理性并不能提供庇护；前者（常识）是一种定义不清、反复无常的能力，它总是在探究和发现面前不堪一击；而后者（理性），恰恰是理性的运用本身，揭示了那些潜藏在现实表面之下的悖论。

  

他刚到牛津时，发现那里完全被战后的语言学风潮所席卷。日常语言学派（ordinary-language movement）让麦基感到困惑，但他的同辈们却以一种胜利者的狂热拥抱它，他们中许多人认为，一旦哲学那本质上起澄清作用的任务完成，其核心问题也就会在分析中烟消云散——因为这些问题之所以显得像问题，仅仅是源于语言的混乱。其结果是，一种与自身的过去奇怪地割裂开来的哲学观诞生了。“太荒谬了，”麦基说，因为“哲学基本上就是它的历史！” 阿尔弗雷德·怀特海（Alfred Whitehead）曾说过，一个人不仅可能在空间上偏狭，也可能在时间上偏狭。而1950年代的牛津则安然地兼具了这两种偏狭，它在历史的康庄大道上，拐进了一条铺着鹅卵石的死胡同，并滋生出一种“小人国”式（Lilliputian）的精神——眼光向内，自鸣得意，近乎自满。

  

> 日常语言学派（Ordinary language philosophy）介绍：https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_language_philosophy

  

20世纪里，陷入一种奇特的、以学科自我设限为特征的内省时期的，并非只有哲学这一个领域，但哲学所呈现出的新的二阶性（second-order character）——用吉尔伯特·赖尔（Gilbert Ryle）的精炼短语来说就是“谈论‘谈论本身’”（talk about talk）——意味着这门学科正在绕开，或者说，正在凌驾于其核心问题之上。一种技术（分析）被误当成了主题；媒介取代了信息。

  

那个时代——如同许多盛极一时又迅速凋零的潮流一样——是一个由极具控制力的人物和其狂热追随者所主导的时代，他们热衷于进行“分析哲学烟火秀”（analytic-pyrotechnics）式的激烈辩论，其目的就是为了展现表演性的才华。为了回顾这类场景，我和麦基观看了一份重要的档案录像——政治理论家杰里·科恩（Jerry Cohen）模仿赖尔（Ryle）的视频，模仿得惟妙惟肖，仿佛赖尔的灵魂附体。（“模仿得‘真是惟妙惟肖’，”麦基笑道。）在他看来——科恩的模仿也暗示了这一点——问题的根源在于，大多数那样的哲学家并没“‘身负’哲学问题，从不曾有过，也根本不知道‘身负’哲学问题是什么感觉。”麦基回忆道：“赖尔是个才华横溢、能启迪生命的人，但他没有什么值得一提的内在生命（inner life）。”（而赖尔本人，想必会欣然同意这一点，他会认为谈论“内在生命”本身就无可救药地陷入了隐喻的混乱之中）。数年后，在耶鲁，麦基发现了康德，更晚些时候又发现了叔本华，从而唤醒了他自身潜藏的康德主义（Kantianism）倾向。太多的哲学家终其一生都在学科的山脚下觅食；而这里，却是如山脉般宏伟的理论。

  

马勒（Mahler）、瓦格纳（Wagner）、书架上的先验形而上学（transcendental metaphysics）、条顿式（Teutonic）的感性以及论述的明晰干净：这一切都让人很难不怀疑麦基骨子里是个德国人；他持久的状态是一种被妥善抑制的存在主义式焦虑，一种永恒的精神上的不得安宁。“嗯，我并不觉得这是一种‘不得安宁’，”他思忖道，“因为如果我对（我的任何一项追求）不感兴趣，那我的生命就缺失了一部分。”

  

如果说那不是不得安宁，麦基确实承认，他感到一种对无限独立、无法抑制的需求，以及对权威的蔑视。（“我不接受权威。”[他笑了]。“我跟它没什么过节。我只是不接受它！”）的确，麦基这种对自己兴趣自觉自愿的投入，似乎例证了一种可能性：一种利己（self-interested）却不自我沉溺（self-absorption）的人生。他曾一度完全放弃工作，转而投身音乐创作，却发现自己的作品矫揉造作得令人难以忍受。“我不是一个伟大的艺术家，但我曾渴望成为……我曾渴望能以诗歌的形式写出伟大的哲学，毕竟有些古希腊人就做到了。但我没有那种天赋。我就是没有。”

  

“我觉得，用纸牌游戏打个比方，我手里分到的是一副好牌——不是一副绝佳的牌，也不是一副顶好的牌——而我想，我这一生的活法，算是把手里的牌打到了极致。但是，我打心底里最希望的，其实是能拿到一副更好的牌。”

  

麦基思想上的漫游，似乎在他三十多岁时进入了最黑暗的阶段，当时他被一场“灾难性力量”的危机所吞噬，表现为一种对死亡无法摆脱的思虑。“我正在浪费我的才华、乃至我的人生的这个念头，开始在我心里扎根。”有好几年，他都生活在一个“无法醒来的噩梦之中，因为我本就醒着”。他的应对之道，是重读哲学经典——并且是“像性命攸关一样去读”，从中搜刮一切可以作为疗愈或解药的洞见。

  

除了早年一段短暂的婚姻，麦基一生独居，这更增添了他的孤独（尽管他的生活“充满了各种人际关系”）。我评论道：“不是每个人都能那么生活”；他回答：“是大多数人不想那么生活。事实上，大多数人不喜欢独处。大多数人害怕独处。”他将伟大的哲学家们视为他“启迪生命的伙伴和向导”，在某些方面，他感觉与他们比与自己的朋友还要亲近。与此形成对照的是，他谈到自己一生都无法回报他人投向他的爱意，这话说得让人有些不安。“我很小就感觉到，我的一个问题不是没人喜欢我，而是我不喜欢他们。”“我年纪越大，就越发意识到，其中一些事情的根源在于我和我母亲的关系。我母亲对我没有一丁点儿母爱，而我知道这一点，因为她总是这么说。”他这场存在主义式的痛苦折磨，最终的产物是一本花了他四年时间写成的小说。这本书最初的书名叫《爱情故事》（Love Story），但后来演变成了《直面死亡》（Facing Death）——毫无疑问，这让销售团队很头疼。

麦基的前同事丹尼斯·希利（Denis Healey）曾谈到，拥有“腹地”（hinterland）在政治上的重要性，而在麦基那严谨细致的牛津风格和“防弹”眼镜背后，他的“腹地”似乎一直延展至一个遥远的消失点。他身上有种维多利亚时代崇高的精神气质，一种专注的凝视，能让所有更轻浮、更世俗的忧虑都为之凋零。这让我想起苏珊·桑塔格（Susan Sontag）曾给博学之才（polymaths）下的一个定义——他们是那种“对一切都感兴趣，且只对一切感兴趣”的人。

When I met him, Bryan Magee was nearly 89, marvellously lucid, curious to hear about my time at Oxford, and paralysed from the waist down: in many ways the ideal interviewee. For a generation of young viewers, Magee’s legendary television series about philosophy were a baptism in the waters of the subject, and he the urbane and worldly gatekeeper to a realm of theoretic abstraction and grounded, vigorous discussion such as had never before been entered—a watershed moment in a primetime slot. “I wouldn’t rely on television for my introduction to anything,” Magee confided in me, blinking from behind glasses so thick they appeared to be double-glazed for warmth. “It seems to me a completely unimportant medium.” _Bryan!_ I wept internally… It was like being told there was really only ever one Ronnie, or watching David Attenborough kick a pigeon. But—remembering what television is generally like—it was hard to disagree. Magee smiled grand-paternally back at me from where he sat, inside a small arms-reach fortress of books and papers, wielding a copy of Dumas the size of house-brick; the television screwed to the opposite wall was, I assumed, purely ornamental. Bryan Magee, who died last month, had a career of intimidating sweep. He came up to Oxford in 1949, a particular post-war moment when the university’s unofficial monopoly in the production of trademark-ruling-elite seems to have been a little overstretched, as Magee found he had to take up several careers almost at once. He was watched by millions as a television journalist on _This Week_ and began his 30-book literary career, making considerable contributions to scholarship on Wagner and Schopenhauer, all the while keeping up friendships with figures like Karl Popper and Bertrand Russell. The television series, _Men of Ideas_ and _The Great Philosophers,_ together took five years to produce, some of which time overlapped with Magee’s ten-year stint as a Labour MP. Given his pick, he would have liked to be foreign secretary, but in 1982 he defected to the SDP, a heavy-hearted move that he knew would result in losing his seat. He had in fact by that stage already discovered that politics “was a way of life that I didn’t really want for myself… I realised—and I think this was an aspect of maturing—that the pursuit of power was in some very deep way a mistake.” The political career was a school-boy ambition that palled in the wake of its own attainment; the television, though, that was just for money. “From the beginning, I was looking for ways of making money that didn’t involve my having a job… which is not very easy to find.” Magee quickly discovered he could earn twice as much working part time as most of his contemporaries could in regular work. “That was my only interest in television: it would earn me some money while I started writing books.” It is curious to note that several of Magee’s most enduring legacies were forced upon him somewhat against his efforts. Aubrey Singer, the notorious broadcasting executive who conceived _Men of Ideas_ in the bath one day_,_ seems to have pursued him over months. Magee continually demurred, eventually agreeing to a pilot with Noam Chomsky, but only if he retained a veto over its being broadcast. “I deliberately chose Chomsky because I thought he would be the most difficult subject to make plain and interesting to a non-philosophical audience.” Actually, Magee confessed, “I’d even created an interim hurdle. I’d said to Aubrey, ‘well, I may make it work once, but I don’t think I can do a whole series of them,’ and Aubrey rather exasperatedly said, ‘all right then, two!’” The two pilots found their elusive target, never sacrificing intellectual integrity to intelligibility; Magee was stunned. Perhaps of even greater lasting importance, though, was _One in Twenty,_ the book Magee wrote about homosexuality after presenting [two](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6PBZQJb_7QI) controversial [editions](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dZMbqThbYV0) of _This Week_ on the subject_._ The book’s direct, unflinching style made it an unforeseen best-seller and “contributed to the change in the law,” the 1967 decriminalisation act of the following year. Again, he admitted to me, “that’s an example like what I was telling you with the philosophy television programmes. I didn’t want to write that, and was talked into it by somebody else” (the publisher, a friend of his, who confided in Magee that he himself was a “practising homosexual,” and implored him to write the book.) It was at first a little odd to hear Magee talk in notes of slight dispassion about these projects, which in anybody else’s life would likely figure as career-defining accomplishments. The two philosophy series—the interviews with figures like Marcuse, and Ayer, and Bernard Williams—seem to me to constitute an indispensable document of imperishable value: at once timeless and dated almost beyond parody. Every episode began with Magee plus guest sitting on a sofa, suspended within what appeared to be an enormous beige womb, or cosmic waiting-room—a kind of philosophic purgatory in three camera-angles. Magee, partly concealed behind his signature welding-goggles, would then deliver a sombre set-piece to camera, mapping out in multi-clausal, donnish cadences, the territory to be covered. Of course, the show’s austere, rather unflashy production actually disguised its considerable presentational intelligence. Magee was the vital element; it became a running joke that he frequently appeared to have a surer grip on his guest’s material than they did, and he described going to some lengths to disguise the degree to which he masterfully steered and clarified the discussion as it developed. Consequently, one of the things that the shows suggest so wonderfully is philosophy’s essentially discursive character. Both Chomsky and Quine would later say that their interviews captured the most direct, general statement of their work.

_**Magee inhabited “a nightmare from which it was impossible to awake"—his response was to re-read the masterpieces of philosophy "as if my life depended on it"**_

  

The shows “wouldn’t be made now. It isn’t imaginable,” said Magee with conviction. “But, people can’t say that it didn’t meet the needs of an audience, because it did. It had record audience figures.” It isn’t, I suppose, a controversial view that educational television today is a genre irretrievably sunk in affectations of style and structure, deformed by gimmick and arcs, and the creative death-grip of decision-by-committee. “The people who are responsible for the change are the programme makers. There’s been an attempt to turn everything into light-entertainment, to turn even the news bulletins into entertainment, which has taken the guts out of television, I think.” So, while it’s not that Magee wasn’t proud of the shows, and with entitlement—he was—they seemed to figure from his point of view as incidental: marginal, if enjoyable, diversions in a life given over to philosophic contemplation. This overturns a serious potential misperception of Magee, one that casts him as some public-spirited pedagogue of high-Reithian vintage, who muttered the BBC Charter in his sleep and martyred his career to the manual eye-opening of the great unread. Magee never ran on that kind of fuel. When, for example, “people say they went into politics because they wanted to make the world a better place, that’s very foreign to me… _I wanted to go into politics because I thought I’d like being in politics_.” His governing, pragmatic rationale was always that he needed an occupation to support his pre-occupation: his life-long grappling with the philosophic problems. Indeed, Magee repeatedly described himself as “_having”_ philosophic problems, as if naming a psychological affliction. As a child he would dwell, solitary and obsessive, on the antinomies of time and space, like an uprooted ancient puzzling the problems through, systemless and unguided. Woven everywhere into the fabric of reality, Magee perceived paradox and palpable mystery. He was “enslaved” by the questions and wonderstruck by the sheer fact of existence. His time as a tutor at Balliol served to reinforce his conviction that philosophical problems are a thing “felt,” requiring a responsiveness of spirit akin to a sensitivity for great art. Not a day went by without such metaphysical puzzles pressing themselves upon his consciousness. At times they tormented him. Once, midway through chapel, he became so overwhelmed by the apprehension of the unbridgeable separation between his mind and the external world that he fled the church, staggering, almost collapsing before a bemused congregation of his peers, who would never have guessed the source of his sickness was not systemic but philosophic. I suggested that such moments of revelation, epiphanic in their force, have been a dominating source of insight for Magee. “Yes. That’s a very good perception. I don’t think of myself as fundamentally an intellectual person.” Questions about the nature of ultimate reality, and what we can know of it seem “obviously the most important and interesting there are,” said Magee, “and in my heart of hearts I do not really understand why not everybody sees them as such.” Appeals to common sense and reason provide no asylum; the former ill-defined and capricious faculty has a record of invariably being contradicted by inquiry and discovery, and it is the application of reason itself that reveals the paradoxes lying barely submerged beneath reality’s surface. Upon his arrival, he found Oxford thoroughly in the grip of its post-war linguistic convulsion. The ordinary-language movement left Magee bewildered, but was embraced with triumphalist zeal by his contemporaries, many of whom held that once philosophy’s essentially clarificatory task was complete, its central problems would have been analysed out of existence—their appearance as problems depending simply on confusions of language. The result was a conception of philosophy curiously cut off from its past. Ridiculous, said Magee, because “philosophy basically _is_ its past!” Alfred Whitehead once observed that it is possible to be provincial in time as well as location, and 1950s Oxford comfortably secured both kinds of parochialism, settling into a cobbled cul-de-sac on the highway of history, and cultivating a Lilliputian spirit that was inward-looking and self-congratulatory bordering on the complacent. While philosophy was not the only field in the 20(th )century to enter a prolonged period of strangely subject-limiting self-awareness, its new second-order character—“talk about talk” in Gilbert Ryle’s compact phrase—meant that the subject was talking past, or rather over, its central questions. A technique, analysis, had been mistaken for subject matter; the medium displacing the message. The era—as many in thrall to a fashion vivid in bloom and quick to expire—was one of domineering personalities with cultish followings, given to pitch battles in analytic-pyrotechnics designed to exhibit brilliance in performance. To commemorate such displays, Magee and I studied an important piece of archival-footage, a [video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=80AovwgVY8Q) of political theorist Jerry Cohen allowing himself to be possessed by the ghost of Ryle. (That’s “really spot on,” laughed Magee.) As he saw it—and Cohen’s impersonation hints—the root of the problem was that most such philosophers didn’t “_have_ philosophic problems and never had had them or any idea what it was like to have them.” “Ryle was a person of life-enhancing intellectual brilliance, but he had no inner life worth speaking of,” Magee recalled. (Ryle, for his part, would no doubt have readily agreed, thinking talk of an inner life hopelessly couched in metaphorical confusion). It was some years later, at Yale, that Magee discovered Kant, and much later Schopenhauer, and awoke to his inchoate Kantianism. So many philosophers spend their lives foraging in the foothills of the subject; here were theories of mountainous grandeur. The Mahler, the Wagner, the shelves of transcendental metaphysics, the Teutonic sensibility and cleanliness of exposition: it’s hard not to suspect Magee of being secretly German; his abiding condition one of well-contained existential anxiety, of permanent spiritual restlessness. “Well, I don’t experience it [as restlessness],” he considered, “because not to be interested in [any one of my pursuits] would be missing a part of my life.” If not restlessness, then Magee did admit to feeling an irrepressible need for unbounded independence, and contempt for authority (“I don’t accept authority. [He laughed]. I don’t have a problem with it. I just don’t accept it!”). Indeed, Magee’s self-conscious devotion to his interests seems to illustrate the possibility of a self-interested life free from self-absorption. At intervals, he gave up work altogether, once to take up musical composition, but found that his compositions were intolerably mawkish. “I’m not a great artist, and I would like to have been… I would like to have written great philosophy in the form of poetry, which after all some of the ancient Greeks did. But I didn’t have it in me. It just wasn’t there.” “I feel, to take an analogy from card games, I was dealt a good hand—not a wonderful hand, not a marvellous hand—and I think I’ve lived in such a way as to make the most of the hand I’ve got. But, what I basically wish more than anything else is that I’d had better cards.” Magee’s intellectual wandering seems to have taken its darkest turn in his mid-30s when he became engulfed by a crisis of “cataclysmic force,” manifesting in an inescapable pre-occupation with death. “The conviction that I was wasting not just my talents but my life began to take root.” For several years he inhabited “a nightmare from which it was impossible to awake because I was awake already.” His response was to re-read the masterpieces of philosophy—and “read them as if my life depended on it,” plundering them for insights that might serve as therapy or cure. To the increase of his isolation, apart from a short, early marriage, Magee always lived alone, (though his life has been “full of relationships”). “Not everyone could live like that,” I remarked; “Most people wouldn’t want to,” he replied. “In fact, most people don’t like being alone. Most people are afraid of being alone.” He regarded the great philosophers as his “life-enhancing companions and guides,” to whom he felt closer, in some respects, than his friends. As counterpoint to this, he spoke, a little disquietingly, of his life-long incapacity to reciprocate the romantic love directed at him. “I felt from an early age that a problem that I had was not that nobody liked me, but that I didn’t like them.” “The older I get, the more I realise that the roots of some of these things lie in my relationship with my mother. My mother had no affection for me whatsoever, and I knew that because she kept saying so.” The upshot of his existential torment was a novel that took four years to write. The book started life under the title _Love Story_ but evolved—much, no doubt, to the distress of the sales team—into _Facing Death._ Denis Healey, Magee’s former colleague, used to talk of the political importance of having “hinterland,” and behind his painstaking Oxonian style and bullet-proof glasses, Magee’s seemed to unfurl towards a vanishing point. There was a kind of Victorian high-mindedness about him, an intensity of gaze that caused more frivolous, worldly concerns to wilt away. I am reminded of a definition Susan Sontag once gave of polymaths—they are people who are interested in everything and nothing else. His unremitting seriousness of purpose, his preoccupation with the grand problems of philosophy—at times beyond what he could bear—the range of material in which he was willing to find insight, all suggest he connected with philosophy more authentically than many of the subject’s professionals. The nature of ultimate reality is more elusive than we tend to admit to ourselves; there is more—much more, perhaps—in heaven and earth than is dreamt of in philosophy. Despite his own predicament, inescapably possessed by this mystery, Magee saw that we are not all so made as to live our lives as he did. One consolation. “Death will be upon us before we know where we are; and once we are dead it will be forever.”
